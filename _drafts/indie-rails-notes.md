# 2) How does crafting a narrative, and deciding what to
exclude and revise, help to serve other people?

For me, a nice backstory has two main impacts:
  - it's an ice breaker: it whets the appetite, gets out of the way, and allows us to focus on more relevant topics
  - it acts as a filter even before we start talking: if you're a team that values engineering degrees, you know from the get go that I'm not a good fit.

I think there is a tension between your history and your story, especially in the world we live in where people expect to wrap their heads around you (your persona) in seconds, YET they also expect you to carry a compelling enough story that they'll want more of you.

For instance, there are two main types of people I wish to talk to:
  - Execs: People who hold the keys of the purse need talking points to make the case for hiring you. We talk about numbers, improvements, KPIs, and such things.
  - Technical/product people: People with whom you’ll work day-in day-out want to verify you're neither an arse, nor a fluke. This requires a wide range of longer form content: personal stories, technical posts...

This is something that evolves constantly. Right now, I want a page dedicated to my freelancing on my website, and I need to cater to these two different types of people. And even with my marketing background, it's tough to do so.

# 3) How is looking for clients different than looking for a regular job?

- In my experience, none. But it has to do with my background. 
- I never had academic or professional credentials that open doors.
- Always relied on my ability to solve problems, to explain the why and the how.
- Getting exposure through building and writing in public to offset my starting the race from a less ideal position.
- Also got involved in French Ruby communitities, even if not a lot, but always trying to be nice and useful.
- So, I ended up with a bit of a presence online, and it opened some doors for me. I've never been able to skip recruitment processes entirely, but it usually makes the first rounds easier. But it helped established another kind of credentials.
- Also, writing and sharing is how I learned to talk while working, which helped me get my first job: my technical test was mediocre at best, but the EM loved how I had expressed my thought process and saw potential. Very grateful for having met that lovely human being hey Thibthib).

- I was interviewing with a company called Drivy (later bought by Getaround), a car-sharing app. And the then CTO told me, in the middle of our first call: "Since you’re a junior developer and have limited skills, we’d give you $some_amount per annum." 
- This made me realized I was not putting my former experiences forward enough. Because I was already saavy in product and marketing, so I could bring very product-and-business oriented opinions to the tech conversations.
- This is where I started talking about my full skillset: writing, design, craftmanship, entrepeneurship, etc...A friend of mine, called Patrick, had me wrote everything I had ever learned in my life, whether professionally or personnally, and from that, I drew skills useful for tech teams.

# 5) You wrote that "freelancing is a whole extra job" on top of the core offering. After that first foray into freelancing, what skills did you build, or what understanding did you have, that made things go more smoothly this time?

- First, I grew up *a lot* since my first freelancing gig. Back then, I had a chip on my shoulder the size of the Pacific, and I was very immature. So, I treated my clients as annoyances to my genius, rather as partners. 
- So, a lot of growing up, seeking professional help, and learning how to be a better human all around. 
- Then, I got better at my core offering. Back then, I was doing marketing stuff but I was 1 year in, so not really good. Now, I'm 8 years in programming, so even if my still a medium-level programmer, I know enough my way around to not have to fake it until i make it.
- Then, I also streamlined my processes/tools. I have a wiki of sort, where I keep track of a lot of things: 
  - each client has a page with their contact, my rates, their quirks... Basically, the first 6 months of feedbacks are stored there
  - I have a time log, where i log all my billables (and none billable) per clients, with links to projects pages, etc... This allows me to generate invoices in a semi automated way (1 hour per month for invoicing)
  - Same things for expenses: a database of all my expenses with properties and receipts, all in one place.
